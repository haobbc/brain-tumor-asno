Thank you, chair. Good morning, everyone.

---

Global neuro-oncology epidemiology — when we cite incidence, demographics, survival — we lean on Western registries. CBTRUS dominates the field.

---

But CBTRUS is American population data. The Asian surgical experience is underrepresented.

---

Genetic, environmental, healthcare-system factors — these all shape what arrives in the operating room.

---

Taiwan has universal national health insurance, an aging population, and a different brain-tumor case mix than Western cohorts.

---

We need our own numbers.

---

We addressed that gap with five years of single-center surgical data from a major tertiary center in Taiwan.

---

3,309 surgeries. 2,875 unique patients. From August 2020 through July 2025.

---

Today I'll show you what aligns with CBTRUS, what doesn't, and why it matters for Asian neuro-oncology.

---

Briefly, the methods.

---

Retrospective review at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital, Linkou branch. One of Taiwan's largest tertiary referral centers.

---

We started with 3,563 surgeries from the operating-room schedule.

---

Two exclusion criteria. First — 57 diagnostic biopsies followed by definitive resection within 100 days, treated as a single case to avoid duplication. Second — 197 cases whose pathology returned non-neoplastic.

---

Final cohort — 3,309 surgeries from 2,875 unique patients.

---

Histopathology coded under both WHO 2016 and 2021 criteria. We adopted CBTRUS grouping conventions to enable direct international comparison.

---

Procedures classified by Taiwan's National Health Insurance codes — resection by tumor size, transsphenoidal pituitary, skull base, and biopsy. A six-category structured taxonomy.

---

The cohort, in three numbers.

---

Mean patient age — 54.2 years, with a standard deviation of 16.7. A predominantly adult population.

---

Over 80% of surgeries performed on patients aged 40 and older. Slight female predominance — 53.9% of unique patients.

---

Mean age varied dramatically by tumor type. Embryonal tumors — mean age 18 years. Germ cell tumors — 19. At the other end, lymphomas — mean 64. Metastatic tumors — 60.

---

And surgical volume grew steadily. 565 cases in year one, 735 in year five — a 30% increase across the study window.

---

That volume growth is reflected here in the year-by-year procedure breakdown.

---

Multiple drivers — improved imaging access, increasing public awareness, aging demographics. Likely all contribute.

---

Now the central finding — the histopathology pie for all 3,309 surgeries.

---

Meningioma grades 1 and 2 lead at 20.6% — 682 cases.

---

Metastatic tumors second at 18.7% — 619 cases. A reminder that systemic cancer remains a major neurosurgical workload.

---

In our cohort, 95.8% of metastatic cases occurred in patients 40 and older — a more pronounced age-skew than reported in Western series. Likely reflects Taiwan's aging cancer population.

---

Pituitary tumors 16.5% — 546 cases.

---

Diffuse glioma grade 4 — glioblastoma — 16.4%, or 543 cases.

---

These four diagnoses account for roughly three-quarters of our surgical workload.

---

Now compare to CBTRUS — but match the denominator first.

---

CBTRUS reports meningioma at 39.7% of all primary CNS tumors. To compare, we exclude metastatic cases — leaving 2,690 primary CNS surgeries. Our meningioma share is 26.6%. Still lower than CBTRUS — many CBTRUS meningiomas are managed conservatively and never reach an operating room.

---

Glioblastoma — CBTRUS 14.2% of all primary CNS tumors. With the same denominator, our share is 20.2%. Higher than CBTRUS — likely reflecting tertiary referral bias toward complex malignant cases.

---

Together — meningioma underrepresented, glioblastoma overrepresented — points to a consistent pattern. Tertiary referral concentrates complex pathology, and conservatively managed benign tumors stay in the community.

---

Pituitary at 16.5% of all surgeries — or 20.3% of primary CNS — is notably high. Consistent with Molitch's community prevalence of 10 to 22%, and consistent with our institution's pituitary expertise.

---

Gender patterns next. Looking at the 1,549 female patients.

---

Meningioma takes 30.2% of female surgeries. Pituitary 17.5%. Metastatic 18.0%.

---

Female-to-male ratio for meningioma grades 1 to 2 — 2.5 to 1. Strong female predominance, consistent with the hormonal hypothesis from Wiemels.

---

Among males — diffuse glioma grade 4 dominates. Male-to-female ratio for glioblastoma higher than CBTRUS — consistent with the literature.

---

Germ cell tumors flip the other way — male-to-female ratio of 3 to 1 in our cohort.

---

Pediatric findings — patients under 15 — show a completely different distribution.

---

Embryonal tumors at 14.8%. Germ cell tumors at 13.6%. Ependymal at 9.1%. Other astrocytic 12.5%.

---

Other benign tumors take the lead at 26.1%. The pediatric panel essentially has no meningioma or pituitary — those are adult diseases.

---

This pediatric pattern broadly matches global data, with germ cell representation notably higher than Western reports — a known East-Asian pattern.

---

Surgical procedure mix.

---

3.1-to-6 centimeter resections dominate — 37.7%, n=1,243.

---

Transsphenoidal pituitary 16.6%. Skull base resection 16.4%. Resections under 3 cm 13.9%. Resections over 6 cm 16.4%. Biopsy only 3.5%.

---

Across all five years, the procedure mix stayed proportional. Volume grew, but structure didn't shift — suggesting stable case-mix and consistent referral patterns.

---

Three limitations before we conclude.

---

One — single-center tertiary referral. Our distribution overweights complex and surgically amenable cases. Community-managed benign tumors are underrepresented.

---

Two — surgical-only cohort. We miss imaging-only diagnoses and conservatively managed lesions — small meningiomas, microadenomas, low-grade gliomas under observation.

---

Three — no molecular data yet. The 2021 WHO classification requires IDH, 1p19q, MGMT for proper glioma stratification. Our retrospective records predate routine documentation.

---

Despite these caveats, three findings warrant attention.

---

Pituitary surgical volume — disproportionately high. Suggests both genuine prevalence and care concentration at expert centers. Implications for workforce planning and pituitary fellowship training.

---

Meningioma female predominance — 2.5 to 1 in our cohort — supports the hormonal hypothesis and motivates ongoing research into hormone-receptor-driven therapeutics.

---

Volume growth without case-mix shift — suggests true incidence increase rather than diagnostic threshold change.

---

Three take-home messages.

---

One — Taiwan's surgical brain-tumor distribution aligns with CBTRUS at the top end, but with two divergences after denominator-matching: meningioma underrepresented, glioblastoma and pituitary overrepresented. A pattern consistent with tertiary referral concentration.

---

Two — surgical volume is growing. 30% over five years. System capacity needs to keep pace.

---

Three — next steps. Multi-center expansion. Molecular profiling integration. Asian registry collaboration. The goal: put Taiwan on the international neuro-oncology map.

---

Thank you. I'm happy to take questions.
