# Abstract

Brain tumors pose a significant public health challenge globally, with varying incidence, treatment outcomes, and disparities in care across populations. This study provides a detailed descriptive analysis of brain tumor surgeries conducted at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch, a major medical center in Taiwan, between August 2020 and July 2025. Through a retrospective review of electronic health records, we analyzed 3,309 surgeries from 2,875 unique patients to elucidate demographic and clinical characteristics, histopathological distributions based on WHO 2016 and 2021 classifications, and surgical approaches. Results revealed meningiomas (20.6%, n=682) and metastatic tumors (18.7%, n=619) as the most common histopathologies, followed by pituitary tumors (16.5%, n=546) and diffuse gliomas grade 4 (16.4%, n=543). Over 80% of surgeries were performed on patients aged 40 years and older, with notable gender disparities, such as a female predominance in meningiomas (71.3% of cases). Surgical volume increased over the study period, from 565 in 2020-2021 to 735 in 2024-2025, with brain tumor resections for tumors sized 3.1-6 cm being the most frequent procedure (37.7%, n=1,243). These findings align with international data from the Central Brain Tumor Registry of the United States (CBTRUS) while highlighting unique patterns potentially influenced by genetic, environmental, or healthcare system factors in Taiwan. Despite limitations, including a single-center focus and exclusion of non-surgical cases, this study underscores the need for specialized care and long-term follow-up strategies. It lays the groundwork for multi-center research and integration of molecular diagnostics to enhance brain tumor management in Taiwan and contribute to global neuro-oncology knowledge.

# Background

### Background

Brain tumors represent a significant public health challenge due to their impact on morbidity and mortality across diverse populations. As the most common solid tumors in children and a leading cause of cancer-related death in this group, and with a substantial burden in adults, particularly for high-grade gliomas and meningiomas, understanding the epidemiology, treatment outcomes, and disparities in care for brain tumors is critical [@price2025CBTRUSStatisticalReport; @ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. These tumors encompass a wide spectrum of histopathological types, ranging from benign to highly malignant, with varying prognoses influenced by factors such as tumor grade, location, molecular characteristics, and patient demographics [@louis20162016WorldHealth]. The World Health Organization (WHO) classifications of 2016 and 2021 have refined diagnostic criteria by integrating molecular markers, such as IDH mutations and 1p/19q co-deletions, into the categorization of gliomas, thereby enhancing precision in diagnosis and prognosis [@louis20162016WorldHealth; @louis2021WHOClassification].

Globally, the incidence of brain and central nervous system (CNS) tumors varies by region, with higher rates reported in developed countries, potentially due to better diagnostic capabilities and access to advanced imaging technologies like MRI [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport; @bondy2008BrainTumorEpidemiology]. In the United States, data from the Central Brain Tumor Registry of the United States (CBTRUS) indicate an overall age-adjusted incidence rate of 24.71 per 100,000 for primary brain and CNS tumors, with non-malignant tumors (e.g., meningiomas) comprising a significant proportion (71.7%) compared to malignant tumors (28.3%) [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. Despite advances in surgical techniques, radiotherapy, and systemic therapies, survival rates for malignant brain tumors, such as glioblastoma, remain dismal, with a median survival of approximately 8-14.6 months even with aggressive treatment [@stupp2005RadiotherapyConcomitantAdjuvant; @lacroix2001MultivariateAnalysis416]. Conversely, benign tumors like WHO Grade I meningiomas often have better outcomes, though recurrence rates vary based on the extent of surgical resection [@nanda2017RelevanceSimpsonGrading; @simpson1957RecurrenceIntracranialMeningiomas].

Significant gaps remain in the understanding of brain tumor epidemiology and treatment outcomes, particularly regarding disparities across demographic groups and healthcare settings. Studies have highlighted variations in incidence and survival by race, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status, with non-Hispanic Black individuals showing higher rates of certain tumors like meningiomas, and disparities in access to care impacting treatment decisions and outcomes [@walsh2025GradestratifiedMeningiomaRisk; @curry2010RacialEthnicSocioeconomic]. Furthermore, the influence of provider volume and institutional expertise on surgical decision-making (e.g., biopsy versus resection) for high-grade gliomas underscores the need for standardized care protocols, especially in older populations [@reiner2025ProviderVolumeImpacts; @viozzi2025BetweenhospitalVariationBiopsy]. While population-based registries like CBTRUS provide robust data on incidence and survival, there is a paucity of research on how these patterns translate to specific clinical contexts, particularly in diverse healthcare systems with varying resource availability.

This study addresses these gaps by providing a detailed descriptive analysis of brain tumor surgeries conducted at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch between 2020 and 2025. By focusing on a cohort of 3,309 surgeries from 2,875 patients, we aim to elucidate the demographic and clinical characteristics, histopathological distributions based on WHO 2016 and 2021 classifications, and surgical approaches employed in a major medical center in Taiwan. This research contributes to the global understanding of brain tumor management by offering insights into treatment patterns and outcomes in a specific Asian population, which may differ from Western cohorts due to genetic, environmental, and healthcare system factors [@chen2016CancerStatisticsChina; @jung2019CancerStatisticsKorea]. Through this analysis, we seek to inform clinical practice and highlight areas for future research into optimizing care for brain tumor patients.

# Methodology

### Methodology

#### Study Design and Research Purpose
This study was designed as a retrospective descriptive analysis to investigate the characteristics and surgical interventions for brain tumor patients over a five-year period from August 1, 2020, to July 31, 2025. The primary purpose was to provide a comprehensive overview of the demographic, clinical, and histopathological profiles of patients undergoing surgical resection or diagnostic biopsy for brain tumors at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch, a major medical center in Taiwan. By analyzing a large cohort of surgical cases, this study aims to contribute to the understanding of brain tumor epidemiology and treatment patterns in a clinical setting, aligning with similar population-based studies such as those reported by the Central Brain Tumor Registry of the United States (CBTRUS) [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport].

#### Study Population and Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria
The study population included patients diagnosed with brain tumors who underwent either surgical resection or diagnostic biopsy at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch during the specified study period. A total of 3,563 surgeries were initially identified from the operation schedule (raw data). Exclusion criteria were applied to refine the dataset for analysis: (a) 57 diagnostic biopsy surgeries followed by surgical interventions within 100 days were excluded to avoid duplication of cases where subsequent resection provided a more definitive histopathological diagnosis; (b) 197 surgeries confirmed as non-tumor diseases (non-tumor histopathology, such as cases initially suspected as tumors on imaging but later diagnosed as non-neoplastic conditions) were also excluded. After exclusions, the final sample comprised 3,309 surgeries performed on 2,875 unique patients, forming the basis of this descriptive report.

#### Data Collection
Data were collected through a systematic review of electronic health records (EHRs) at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital. The collected variables included patients’ demographic characteristics (e.g., age, sex), clinical characteristics (e.g., presenting symptoms, preoperative status), and histopathological diagnoses. This approach ensured a comprehensive dataset for analysis, consistent with methodologies used in large-scale cancer registry studies [@jung2019CancerStatisticsKorea; @ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport].

#### Histopathological Classification
Histopathological diagnoses were classified based on the 2016 and 2021 World Health Organization (WHO) Classifications of Tumours of the Central Nervous System, which provide standardized criteria for tumor grading and typing [@louis20162016WorldHealth; @louis20212021WHOClassification]. Additionally, the grouping approaches from the CBTRUS Statistical Reports for Primary Brain and Other Central Nervous System Tumors Diagnosed in the United States (versions 2017-2021 and 2018-2022) were adopted with minor modifications to ensure comparability with international data [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. For gliomas, most histological entities were categorized according to WHO grade, with specific groups such as "Other Astrocytic Gliomas" reserved for certain subtypes. Due to small sample sizes, Ependymal Tumors were grouped irrespective of WHO grading. Pathological groups with limited sample numbers, such as neurofibromas (classified as tumors of nerves) and craniopharyngiomas (classified as tumors in the sellar region), were aggregated into a universal category labeled "Other Benign Tumors" to facilitate meaningful statistical analysis.

#### Classification of Surgical Procedures
Surgical procedures were categorized based on the payment items defined by Taiwan’s National Health Insurance (NHI) system, ensuring consistency in procedural documentation. The classifications included:
- Brain tumor resection, size ≤3 cm (procedure code 83017B);
- Brain tumor resection, size 3.1-6 cm (procedure code 83018B);
- Brain tumor resection, size >6 cm (procedure code 83019B);
- Transsphenoidal removal of pituitary tumors (procedure code 83057B(A));
- Skull base tumor surgery (procedure code 83088B);
- Biopsy, identified by the presence of procedure codes 83081B, 83082B, or 83010B alone.

For surgeries with other procedure codes not listed above, they were classified under the group "Brain tumor resection, size ≤3 cm" for research purposes. In cases where multiple procedure codes were recorded for a single surgical report, a priority hierarchy was applied as follows: 83088B (skull base tumor surgery), 83057B (transsphenoidal removal), 83019B (resection >6 cm), 83018B (resection 3.1-6 cm), and finally 83017B (resection ≤3 cm). This structured classification aligns with clinical practices and facilitates analysis of surgical intervention types, similar to methodologies used in studies evaluating procedural variations [@reiner2025ProviderVolumeImpacts].

#### Ethical Considerations
This study adhered to ethical principles for medical research involving human subjects as outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki [@2013WorldMedicalAssociation]. All data were anonymized and extracted from electronic health records with appropriate institutional approvals to ensure patient confidentiality and compliance with data protection regulations.

#### Data Analysis
Descriptive statistics were used to summarize the demographic, clinical, and histopathological characteristics of the study population. The analysis focused on the frequency and distribution of tumor types, surgical interventions, and patient characteristics across the cohort. This methodological approach is consistent with large-scale epidemiological studies of brain tumors, providing a foundation for potential future comparative analyses with international datasets [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport; @jung2019CancerStatisticsKorea]. Specific statistical tests or software used for data processing will be detailed in subsequent sections of the research paper as needed for deeper analysis.

#### Reproducibility
To ensure reproducibility, the study methodology, including data collection protocols, exclusion criteria, and classification systems, has been documented in detail. The use of standardized classifications such as the WHO criteria and NHI procedure codes allows for replication of this study in other settings or comparison with other institutional datasets. The electronic health record system at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital provides a reliable and consistent data source for future validation or longitudinal studies.

This methodology provides a robust framework for understanding the characteristics and surgical management of brain tumor patients at a major medical center, contributing valuable insights to the field of neuro-oncology and supporting clinical decision-making and policy development.

# Results

### Results

This section presents the findings from a descriptive study of brain tumor surgeries conducted at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch between August 1, 2020, and July 31, 2025. The results are organized into demographic and clinical characteristics of patients and surgeries, histopathological distributions, and types of surgical interventions performed during the study period.

#### Demographic and Histopathological Distribution of Brain Tumor Surgeries
A total of 3,309 brain tumor surgeries from 2,875 unique patients were analyzed. The distribution of surgeries across histopathological groups by gender and age is detailed in Table 1. Among the surgeries, diffuse astrocytic and oligodendroglial tumors accounted for a significant proportion, with diffuse glioma grade 4 being the most frequent subtype at 16.4% (n=543). Meningiomas (grade 1-2) represented 20.6% (n=682) of surgeries, followed by metastatic tumors at 18.7% (n=619) and pituitary tumors at 16.5% (n=546). Age-wise, the majority of surgeries (80.8%, n=2,674) were performed on patients aged 40 years and older, with a relatively balanced gender distribution across most histopathological groups. Notably, embryonal tumors and germ cell tumors were predominantly observed in patients under 15 years of age, with 73.7% (n=14) and 51.7% (n=15) of respective cases in females within this age group (Table 1).

#### Patient Characteristics at First Surgery
Focusing on the first surgery for each of the 2,875 patients, the histopathological distribution mirrored that of the total surgeries, with meningiomas (grade 1-2) being the most common at 22.8% (n=656), followed by metastatic tumors at 19.6% (n=562) and pituitary tumors at 18.1% (n=520) (Table 2). The age distribution at first surgery showed that 81.7% (n=2,347) of patients were aged 40 years and older, with a slight female predominance (53.9%, n=1,549) across the cohort. Gender-specific differences were evident in certain tumor types, such as meningiomas (grade 1-2), where females accounted for 71.3% (n=468) of cases (Table 2).

#### Detailed Demographic Analysis by Histopathology
Further demographic analysis revealed variations in age and gender ratios across histopathological groups (Table 3). The overall mean age of patients was 54.2 years (SD=16.7), with a median age of 56.8 years (Q1-Q3: 44.3-66.2). Patients with embryonal tumors and germ cell tumors were notably younger, with mean ages of 18.3 years (SD=22.3) and 19.0 years (SD=10.5), respectively. In contrast, patients with lymphomas and metastatic tumors were older, with mean ages of 64.5 years (SD=11.1) and 60.2 years (SD=11.0), respectively. Gender ratios varied significantly, with germ cell tumors showing a male-to-female ratio of 3.00, while meningiomas (grade 1-2) had a ratio of 0.40, indicating a strong female predominance (Table 3).

#### Types of Surgical Interventions Over Time
The distribution of surgical procedures performed during the study period is summarized in Table 4. Of the 3,309 surgeries, brain tumor resection for tumors sized 3.1-6 cm was the most common procedure, accounting for 37.7% (n=1,243) of cases. Transsphenoidal removal of pituitary tumors and brain tumor resection for tumors larger than 6 cm followed, representing 16.6% (n=550) and 16.4% (n=541) of surgeries, respectively. Resection for tumors sized ≤3 cm accounted for 13.2% (n=436), skull base tumor surgery accounted for 12.8% (n=422), and biopsies were the least frequent, comprising 3.5% (n=117) of all procedures. Over the five-year period, a gradual increase in the total number of surgeries was observed, from 565 in August 2020–July 2021 to 735 in August 2024–July 2025. The proportion of brain tumor resections for tumors sized 3.1-6 cm remained consistently high across all years, while the proportion of transsphenoidal removals peaked at 19.7% (n=124) in August 2021–July 2022 (Table 4).

# Discussion

### Discussion

This study provides a comprehensive descriptive analysis of brain tumor surgeries conducted at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch, a major medical center in Taiwan, over a five-year period from August 2020 to July 2025. By examining 3,309 surgeries across 2,875 patients, our findings contribute to the understanding of brain tumor epidemiology and surgical patterns in a Taiwanese context, offering a basis for comparison with international data. The discussion below interprets these results in light of global trends, highlights key differences in tumor distribution and demographic patterns, addresses the study's limitations, and suggests directions for future research.

#### Comparison of Brain Tumor Distribution with International Data
Our study reveals a distribution of brain tumor histopathologies that aligns with, yet diverges from, international reports. Meningiomas (21.6% of surgeries, with Grade 1-2 at 20.6%) and metastatic tumors (18.7%) were among the most frequent, consistent with findings from the Central Brain Tumor Registry of the United States (CBTRUS), where meningiomas account for approximately 39.7% of primary brain and CNS tumors and metastatic tumors are also significant [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. However, the proportion of gliomas, particularly diffuse gliomas of Grade 4 (16.4%), is lower than reported in CBTRUS data, where glioblastomas alone constitute 14.2% of all tumors but over 50% of malignant cases. This discrepancy may reflect a referral bias towards more complex or benign cases at our tertiary center, as well as potential differences in population genetics or environmental risk factors in Taiwan compared to the United States [@bondy2008BrainTumorEpidemiology].

Pituitary tumors (16.5%) also emerged as a significant category in our cohort, aligning with prevalence estimates of 10-22% in community studies as reported by Molitch [@molitch2017DiagnosisTreatmentPituitary]. This high proportion may be attributed to the specialized neurosurgical expertise and advanced imaging capabilities at our center, facilitating the diagnosis and surgical management of these tumors. In contrast, rarer histopathologies such as embryonal tumors (0.6%) and germ cell tumors (0.9%) were less frequent, mirroring global trends where these are predominantly pediatric and less common overall [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport].

#### Increasing Surgical Volume Over Time
A notable finding is the progressive increase in surgical volume over the study period, from 565 surgeries in 2020-2021 to 735 in 2024-2025 (Table 4). This trend may reflect improvements in diagnostic capabilities, increased public awareness of neurological symptoms, and enhanced access to specialized care at our institution. It also parallels global patterns of rising brain tumor diagnoses due to advancements in imaging technologies such as MRI, as noted in historical analyses of incidence rates [@wrensch2002EpidemiologyPrimaryBrain]. However, this increase could also indicate a growing burden of brain tumors in Taiwan, necessitating further investigation into environmental or lifestyle factors contributing to this trend, similar to observations in other Asian countries like Korea [@jung2019CancerStatisticsKorea].

#### Demographic and Histopathological Variations Compared to International Data
The demographic distribution of brain tumor surgeries in our cohort highlights significant age and gender differences that vary from international norms. Over 80% of surgeries were performed on patients aged 40 years and older (Table 1), consistent with the age-specific incidence peaks for meningiomas and gliomas reported in CBTRUS data [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. However, the predominance of metastatic tumors in older age groups (95.8% in ≥40 years) is particularly pronounced in our study, potentially reflecting a higher burden of systemic cancers in Taiwan's aging population compared to Western cohorts [@chen2016CancerStatisticsChina].

Gender disparities also emerged, with females undergoing more surgeries for meningiomas (Grade 1-2: 682 vs. 486 in males) and pituitary tumors (546 vs. 282 in males), aligning with global evidence of a female preponderance in these tumor types due to hormonal influences [@wiemels2010EpidemiologyEtiologyMeningioma; @molitch2017DiagnosisTreatmentPituitary]. Conversely, males showed a higher incidence of diffuse gliomas (Grade 4: 248 vs. 543 in females), consistent with CBTRUS findings of male predominance in malignant gliomas [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. These patterns suggest that while some gender differences are universal, local factors such as healthcare-seeking behavior or genetic predispositions may modulate their expression in Taiwan.

#### Limitations of the Study
Despite its comprehensive scope, this study has several limitations that must be acknowledged. First, as a single-center study conducted at a major medical center, our findings may not fully represent the broader Taiwanese population. Tertiary centers like ours often attract complex cases, such as gliomas or challenging tumors requiring specialized expertise, potentially overrepresenting malignant or surgically amenable tumors while underrepresenting benign tumors managed conservatively in community settings [@bondy2008BrainTumorEpidemiology]. This referral bias limits the generalizability of our results to the national level.

Second, our inclusion criteria focused exclusively on patients with histopathologically confirmed diagnoses through surgery. This approach excludes patients diagnosed via imaging alone or those managed non-surgically, likely underestimating the incidence of benign tumors such as meningiomas or pituitary adenomas that may not require operative intervention [@molitch2017DiagnosisTreatmentPituitary]. This methodological constraint highlights the need for broader data collection strategies to capture the full spectrum of brain tumor epidemiology in Taiwan.

Finally, while our study provides detailed demographic and histopathological data, it lacks information on molecular markers (e.g., IDH mutations, 1p/19q codeletions) that are increasingly critical for prognosis and treatment planning under the 2021 WHO classification [@louis20212021WHOClassification]. The absence of such data limits our ability to align fully with contemporary diagnostic paradigms and assess their impact on surgical outcomes.

#### Implications and Future Research Directions
The findings of this study have important implications for clinical practice and public health policy in Taiwan. The high proportion of meningiomas and pituitary tumors underscores the need for specialized neurosurgical and endocrinological expertise, as well as long-term follow-up strategies to monitor recurrence, particularly for meningiomas where recurrence rates can be significant even after complete resection [@nanda2017RelevanceSimpsonGrading]. Additionally, the increasing surgical volume suggests a growing demand for resources and infrastructure to manage brain tumor patients, necessitating strategic planning to ensure equitable access to care.

Future research should aim to address the limitations identified. Multi-center studies incorporating data from community hospitals and non-surgical cases are essential to provide a more representative picture of brain tumor epidemiology in Taiwan. Integrating molecular profiling into routine clinical practice and research datasets will align local data with international standards, facilitating comparative analyses and personalized treatment approaches [@ostrom2022CBTRUSStatisticalReport]. Furthermore, exploring environmental and genetic risk factors specific to the Taiwanese population could elucidate the observed differences in tumor distribution and incidence trends compared to Western cohorts [@bondy2008BrainTumorEpidemiology].

# Conclusion

# Conclusion

This study offers a detailed examination of brain tumor surgeries at Chang Gung Memorial Hospital Linkou Branch in Taiwan from 2020 to 2025, analyzing 3,309 surgeries across 2,875 patients. Key findings reveal meningiomas (20.6%), metastatic tumors (18.7%), and pituitary tumors (16.5%) as the predominant histopathological types, with over 80% of surgeries performed on patients aged 40 and older. Gender disparities were evident, with females more frequently affected by meningiomas and pituitary tumors. A notable increase in surgical volume over the five-year period highlights a growing demand for specialized care, potentially driven by enhanced diagnostic capabilities and public awareness. While aligning with international trends from the Central Brain Tumor Registry of the United States (CBTRUS), our results also suggest unique patterns influenced by local genetic, environmental, or healthcare factors. These insights emphasize the need for specialized neurosurgical expertise and long-term follow-up strategies in Taiwan. Despite limitations such as a single-center focus and lack of molecular data, this research provides a foundation for future multi-center studies and the integration of advanced diagnostics to improve brain tumor management and contribute to global neuro-oncology knowledge.
